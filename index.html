Basic Information

Inferno (Italian: [iɱˈfɛrno]; Italian for "Hell") is the first part of Italian writer Dante Alighieri's 14th-century epic poem Divine Comedy. It is followed by Purgatorio and Paradiso. The Inferno describes Dante's journey through Hell, guided by the ancient Roman poet Virgil. In the poem, Hell is depicted as nine concentric circles of torment located within the Earth; it is the "realm ... of those who have rejected spiritual values by yielding to bestial appetites or violence, or by perverting their human intellect to fraud or malice against their fellowmen".

Dante is considered the father of the Italian Language and the Divine Comedy is considered one of the greatest works of Italy and the World. Everything was once written in Latin, but Dante wrote in that times' Vernacular.

The Divine Comedy is a journey through hell, purgatory and eventually heaven all while Dante is in search of his love, Beatrice.

It is the most vivid depiction of Hell ever conceived.

The number 3 is utilized multiple times throughout as a mockery of the holy trinity, such as the Devine Comedy itself being in 3 books.

Cantos are sections of a long poem. Inferno has 33 while the entire Devine Comedy has 100.

In Dante's Hell there are 9 sections or 'circles'. In some of these sections, there are Bolgias or rings that represent a sub-set of each sin. Each has a gruesome and horrid punishment.

An Allegory is a story, poem, or picture that can be interpreted to reveal a hidden message which is henerally a moral one.

Canto III

Dante and Virgil pass through the gate of hell, which bears an inscription that reads "Lasciate ogne speranza, voi ch'intrate", most commonly translated to "Abandon all hope, ye who enter here." Here, they also hear the screams of those who were uncommitted, nither evil nor good, just concerned with themselves. After passing through the vestibule, Dante and Virgil reach the ferry that will take them across the river Acheron and to Hell proper. The ferry is piloted by Charon, who does not want to let Dante enter, for he is a living being. Virgil forces Charon to take him by declaring, Vuolsi così colà dove si puote / ciò che si vuole ("It is so willed there where is power to do / That which is willed")
